Systematic Learning: From Track to Algorithms

Breaking down triple jump technique taught me how to approach dynamic programming.

Published:
January 2025
Categories:
Methodology, Computer Science
Series:
Learning Systems (Part 1 of 3)

The Pattern in the Jump

My track coach would stand at the end of the runway with a measuring tape and a notebook. He'd mark positions with chalk. We'd drill the penultimate step over and over—three lefts, three rights, until the muscle memory was automatic. Nothing about triple jump was intuitive. Every phase was deliberate, measured, repeated.

Years later, sitting in CSC 349 staring at a dynamic programming problem, I recognized the same structure. The problem wasn't mystical. It just needed to be broken down.

How You Learn a Jump

Triple jump has four phases: approach, hop, step, jump. Each phase has its own mechanics. The approach isn't just running—it's building controlled speed while maintaining posture. The hop requires a specific knee angle and arm position. The step needs a penultimate adjustment that feels completely unnatural at first.

You don't just "do" a triple jump. You drill the hop in isolation. A hundred times. You work on the approach without the jump. You practice the penultimate step until your body remembers it without thinking. Then you start combining pieces. Approach and hop. Hop and step. Eventually, the full sequence.

My coach would watch, make small corrections, and we'd repeat. The feedback loop was constant.

How You Learn an Algorithm

Longest Common Subsequence seemed impossible at first. The recursion didn't make sense. The table was confusing. The backtracking felt arbitrary.

So I broke it down. First, I just worked on understanding the recurrence relation. Not the full problem—just that one piece. Then I practiced building the table by hand. Multiple times, with different inputs, until the pattern became clear. The backtracking came later, once the table made sense.

Test cases were my coach. Each one showed me where my understanding was off. I'd adjust and try again.

The Time Question

I trained 20-30 hours per week for track. Now I study around 40 hours per week for upper-division CS courses. People sometimes assume the time investment is different for "natural" athletes versus students. It's not. The methodology is the same.

The difference is that I learned early in athletics that progress comes from deliberate repetition. You don't get better at triple jump by thinking about it. You get better by doing the drills. You isolate the hard parts. You practice them until they're not hard anymore.

It took me longer to apply this to academics. When I dropped out of Cal Poly in 2009, I didn't know how to study. I thought intelligence meant things should come easily. By the time I came back in 2024, I understood that learning is systematic. It requires time, repetition, and breaking down complexity into manageable pieces.

What Transfers

The approach works for anything complex. Dance, algorithms, system design, writing. Find the difficult part. Isolate it. Practice it until it's not difficult. Then integrate it back into the whole.

This isn't revolutionary. Anders Ericsson documented deliberate practice decades ago. But understanding it intellectually is different from applying it consistently. Athletics taught me to apply it. Now it's just how I learn.
