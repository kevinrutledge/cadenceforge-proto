The Return to Cal Poly

Dropped out in 2008. Back in 2024 with a different approach.

Published:
December 2024
Categories:
Personal
Series:
The Return (Part 1 of series)

2008

I arrived at Cal Poly as a recruited track athlete with a 790 math SAT score. The combination felt right. I could compete and study engineering. What I didn't understand was how much time engineering coursework actually required.

In high school, I'd done homework during class or lunch. Maybe an hour at home. That approach worked fine. At Cal Poly, it didn't. I failed everything Fall quarter. Because I failed, I couldn't compete freshman year despite being recruited specifically for track. I was still training 20-30 hours per week with no competitive outlet.

By Spring quarter I was passing classes, but barely. The university told me I needed to take significantly more units to make up for Fall. I looked at the situation honestly. I wasn't ready for Cal Poly's pace or tuition. I dismissed myself after freshman year.

The Years Between

I tried community college. Enrolled in calculus, physics, chemistry. Dropped integral calculus multiple times when it got difficult. Same with general chemistry. I was avoiding the discomfort rather than working through it.

I coached track for a while. Developed training programs. Worked at Milestone Technologies doing warehouse logistics, then QA, then analytics. Started ballroom dancing and competed at nationals. Went back to school for kinesiology. Did a data analytics bootcamp. Tried to find a shortcut to a technical career.

None of it was wasted time, but I kept circling back to the same realization. I needed to finish the CS degree properly. No shortcuts. Just the work.

What Changed

At San Jose State for kinesiology, something shifted. I started treating coursework the way I'd treated athletic training. Allocate the time. Break down the material. Practice until it sticks. I got a 3.85 GPA.

When I went back to De Anza and Foothill for CS prerequisites, I registered for the hardest calculus professor available. If I could pass under difficult conditions, I'd be ready for anything. I passed Calc II with a B. Calc III, Calc IV, Linear Algebra, all As. Data structures and algorithms, A.

The material wasn't easier. I was approaching it differently. More time, more repetition, less expectation that things should come quickly.

2024

I got into multiple UCs and Cal Poly again. Chose Cal Poly. Same school, different student. My 2008 transcript is still there—those failing grades don't disappear. But Fall 2024 I got a 3.9 GPA and made Dean's List.

The coursework is demanding. Database Systems, Algorithms, Ethics and AI, plus Hack4Impact development work. But I know how to study now. I allocate 40+ hours per week. I break down complex material into pieces I can practice. I don't expect things to be easy.

I'm 36. Most of my classmates are 20. The age difference is obvious in group work sometimes. But the systematic approach to learning that took me years to develop works. That's what matters.

What I'd Tell 2008 Me

The problem wasn't that you couldn't do the work. The problem was thinking you shouldn't have to work that hard. Intelligence doesn't mean things come easily. It means you can figure out how to learn difficult things if you're willing to put in the time.

Also, stop dropping classes when they get hard. That's when the learning actually starts.
